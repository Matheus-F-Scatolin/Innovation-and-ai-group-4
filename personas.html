Proto-Personas — AI & Homework Integrity Project (v2)

Nine assumption-based proto-personas mapping the people caught in the AI-and-homework-integrity problem. Pick a card to open its one-pager.

Status

Proto-personas (assumption-based). Every claim is a hypothesis to be validated or killed in customer discovery interviews. Do not treat these as market facts yet.

Problem statement

High school teachers who assign out-of-class work struggle with students bypassing the cognitive effort required for learning, because AI allows them to submit work without real comprehension — and they want to ensure students are actually learning.

Market scope (team decision)

Mexico first — good private preparatorias/bachilleratos (ages 15–18), not necessarily elite. Entry via Emiliano's network.

Profesora Lourdes

Instituto Alborada (Beatriz's)

"Yo no le tengo miedo a la inteligencia artificial. Le tengo miedo a graduar generaciones que nunca tuvieron una idea propia."
Age
56
School
Instituto Alborada (Beatriz's)
Subject
Literatura y Historia de México
Experience
28 years teaching; deeply respected, somewhat feared
Load
4 groups × ~35 students
Personality
Principled, formal, proud of her craft; feels the profession is under attack
Tech comfort
Low — uses the school's LMS only because it's mandatory; grades on paper

Goals

  • Restore trust: know with certainty that what she reads was thought by the student.
  • Protect the intellectual rigor of her subject — essays and deep reading must survive.
  • Finish her career with dignity, not as a "content moderator."
  • Have the school back her up with real, enforceable consequences.

Frustrations

  • She can feel AI-written text instantly but can prove nothing — and unprovable accusations have blown up with parents before.
  • In-class handwritten essays consumed the class time she used for discussion — her workaround is cannibalizing her actual teaching.
  • Younger colleagues who "embrace AI" strike her as surrendering; she feels isolated and old.
  • The school has no policy, so she's the "strict one" and students route their complaints around her.

Context

Lourdes built her career on the essay: reading a student's writing was how she knew them. Since 2023 that channel feels poisoned. She experiences AI not as a tool problem but as a betrayal of the pact between teacher and student. Her response has been to retreat: everything important now happens on paper, in class, under her eyes.

Current behavior & workarounds

  • Moved ~80% of grading weight to handwritten, in-class work.
  • Refuses to grade anything typed at home; assigns less homework overall (and knows students read less as a result).
  • Occasionally interrogates suspected students orally — wins the battle, poisons the relationship.

Tech stack & influence

  • Uses: paper, whiteboard, mandated LMS (minimally), personal WhatsApp for parent groups.
  • Adoption role: Vetoer. She won't champion a tool, but her seniority means a tool she publicly rejects is dead in the sala de maestros. A tool she merely tolerates can survive.

Assumptions to validate

  1. Resisters are a large share of the teacher population (not a shrinking fringe) — this determines whether the product must win them or merely neutralize them.
  2. Retreat-to-paper is the dominant resister workaround, and teachers recognize its pedagogical cost.
  3. A resister would accept a tool framed as "restoring trust / proving authorship" even if she rejects anything framed as "integrating AI."

Empathy map

🧠 Think & feel
  • What really counts: students who can think for themselves; the craft and dignity of teaching.
  • Preoccupations: she can no longer trust anything typed; every good essay is now a question mark.
  • Worries & aspirations: graduating a generation without ideas of its own; finishing her career with dignity, with the essay intact.
👂 Hear
  • Influencers: media alarm about AI destroying education — it confirms what she already believes.
  • Boss: Beatriz's AI-ban memo — a rule she agrees with but is given no means to enforce.
  • Friends/colleagues: veteran peers trading suspicions; younger teachers saying "adapt or die," which she hears as an attack.
👀 See
  • Environment: stacks of handwritten pages; class hours consumed by supervised writing instead of discussion.
  • Peers: Renata-types embracing AI; other colleagues quietly giving up on homework altogether.
  • Market offers: detector and "AI literacy" vendors — she distrusts both.
🗣️ Say & do
  • Toward others: formal and demanding with students; more interrogative than she used to be (oral spot-checks that poison relationships).
  • In public: the "strict one" in the sala de maestros; openly defends rigor, publicly rejects AI tools.
  • Appearance: formal, traditional; paper gradebook under her arm — the analog teacher, on purpose.
🛑 Pain
  • Fears: making an accusation she can't prove and having it blow up with parents; ending her career as a "content moderator."
  • Frustrations: she can feel AI text instantly but can prove nothing; her workaround cannibalizes her own teaching time.
  • Obstacles: no school policy with teeth, no evidence mechanism, growing isolation from younger colleagues.
🏆 Gain
  • Wants/needs: certainty that what she reads was thought by the student; trust restored without becoming a police officer.
  • Measures of success: essays she can grade at face value again; being respected rather than routed around.
  • Obstacles to overcome: proving authorship without detectors, oral trials, or giving up take-home writing.

41

Colegio del Roble (Patricia's) + a second, similar school across town
Subject
Matemáticas y Física
Experience
15 years; also tutors privately for extra income
Load
6 groups across two schools (~200 students)
Personality
Practical, tired, allergic to ideology; "just tell me what works"
Tech comfort
Moderate-high — Google Classroom, GeoGebra, Excel gradebook, has tried ChatGPT himself

Goals

  • Know which students can actually solve problems before the exam exposes them — early warning, not autopsy.
  • Keep homework pedagogically useful (practice matters in math) without pretending its grades mean anything.
  • Contain his workload: 200 students across two schools leaves zero slack for oral checks or process review.
  • Anything he adopts must save time net-net within two weeks or it's out.

Frustrations

  • Homework has become theater: he assigns it, AI solves it, he pretends to grade it.
  • The students who most need practice are exactly the ones outsourcing it — he watches them fail exams predictably and can't intervene earlier.
  • "Show your work" is dead: AI shows beautiful work.
  • Tools pitched at his school are always essay/plagiarism tools — nobody builds for math.

Context

Photo-solver apps broke math homework before ChatGPT — Andrés has been fighting this war longer than the humanities teachers. Any problem set he sends home comes back perfect and means nothing. He's not angry anymore; he's rebalancing: homework barely counts, in-class exams count for almost everything. He knows this measures less (no long projects, no multi-step modeling) but it's honest.

Current behavior & workarounds

  • Homework weight cut to 10%; exams and in-class quizzes carry the grade.
  • Randomizes numeric values per student on some worksheets (manual, painful, only slows students down for minutes).
  • Quick "explain step 3 to me" spot-checks when he smells outsourcing — effective, doesn't scale past a handful of students.

Tech stack & influence

  • Uses: Google Classroom, GeoGebra, Excel, WhatsApp, ChatGPT (for making exercises — an irony he acknowledges).
  • Adoption role: Pragmatic influencer. Won't fight for a tool, but if something demonstrably saves him time, he uses it and colleagues copy him. Fast to abandon anything with friction.

Assumptions to validate

  1. STEM teachers' pain is early visibility into who is actually practicing, not authorship-proof (different product than for humanities).
  2. The "homework is now theater" rebalancing (weight → exams) is widespread in math/science.
  3. A net time-saving bar within ~2 weeks is the real adoption threshold for high-load teachers.
  4. Dual-school, ~200-student loads are common enough that "doesn't scale past 20 students" kills a feature.

Empathy map

🧠 Think & feel
  • What really counts: knowing who can actually solve problems; honest measurement; protecting his scarce time.
  • Preoccupations: 200 students across two schools; homework that has become theater he still has to stage.
  • Worries & aspirations: watching students fail exams predictably without being able to intervene earlier; he wants early warning, not autopsy.
👂 Hear
  • Influencers: math-teacher WhatsApp/Facebook groups — everyone tells the same photo-solver war stories.
  • Boss: two coordinators with two different (and contradictory) AI attitudes; neither offers him anything usable.
  • Friends/colleagues: colleagues who quietly rebalanced everything to exams, same as he did.
👀 See
  • Environment: perfect homework that means nothing; gradebooks full of numbers he doesn't believe.
  • Peers: the whole department pretending to grade what AI pretended to solve.
  • Market offers: essay/plagiarism tools pitched at his school — nothing built for math.
🗣️ Say & do
  • Toward others: blunt, practical, fair; quick "explain step 3 to me" spot-checks when he smells outsourcing.
  • In public: the no-ideology pragmatist — "just tell me what works"; complains little, adapts fast.
  • Appearance: tired but reliable; the teacher who is always between two schools, laptop and Excel gradebook.
🛑 Pain
  • Fears: that the students who most need practice keep outsourcing it and fail on schedule; that anything new eats his non-existent slack.
  • Frustrations: "show your work" is dead — AI shows beautiful work; grading theater; manual value-randomizing that buys minutes.
  • Obstacles: zero time for oral checks at 200 students; no tools designed for STEM; homework weight already cut to 10%.
🏆 Gain
  • Wants/needs: early visibility into who is actually practicing, before the exam exposes them.
  • Measures of success: net time saved within two weeks of adopting anything; fewer "predictable" exam failures.
  • Obstacles to overcome: making homework a real signal again without adding a single hour to his week.

English (B1–C1 groups; school sells bilingual certification to parents)
Experience
5 years; did a TESOL certificate, active in teacher communities online
Load
5 groups × ~30 students
Personality
Energetic, experimental, slightly contrarian; the "AI teacher" of her school
Tech comfort
High — builds her own materials with AI, runs a class Notion, early adopter of everything

Goals

  • Make AI-resistant (or AI-inclusive) assignments sustainable — right now her method costs her double the prep and grading time.
  • Prove to colleagues and dirección that her approach produces real language gains, with evidence.
  • Become the school's AI lead — she sees a career in it.
  • Give students a legitimate way to use AI (as tutor/feedback) without it doing the work for them.

Frustrations

  • Reviewing process (drafts, revisions, recordings) for 150 students is crushing — she's the proof that the "right" pedagogy doesn't scale unaided.
  • Colleagues dismiss her as naive; Lourdes-types treat her as part of the problem.
  • No institutional policy means her AI-permissive rules contradict the AI-ban rules next door — students exploit the inconsistency.
  • Existing tools are detectors (which she thinks are snake oil) — nobody sells her what she actually needs: process visibility and scalable oral/formative assessment.

Context

Renata thinks the war is over and AI won — so the assignment has to change, not the student. She's redesigned her homework around process and performance: drafts with visible revision, recorded voice notes, in-class conversations about what students wrote. Her problem isn't philosophy, it's logistics: her redesigned assessment doesn't scale, and the school gives her no time, no tools, and no cover when parents ask why her class "uses the cheating machine."

Current behavior & workarounds

  • Requires Google Docs with version history; spot-checks revision timelines (tedious, gameable).
  • Voice-note assignments graded during her commute.
  • Wrote her own class AI policy ("use it to critique your draft, never to write it") — unenforceable, but sets culture.

Tech stack & influence

  • Uses: Google Workspace, Notion, ChatGPT/Claude for materials, Vocaroo/WhatsApp voice notes, Kahoot.
  • Adoption role: Champion. She will pilot anything promising, evangelize it, and drag her school into a trial. The natural design partner and internal seller — but she has no budget authority.

Assumptions to validate

  1. Embracers exist in meaningful numbers in Mexican private schools and self-identify quickly (they're the beachhead if so).
  2. The embracer's bottleneck is genuinely scaling process-based assessment, not policy or conviction.
  3. A champion-teacher → director sales motion works (Renata can get a pilot approved).
  4. "Legitimate AI use" framing (tutor, not ghostwriter) resonates with parents rather than alarming them.

Empathy map

🧠 Think & feel

  • Preoccupations: drowning in drafts, revisions, and voice notes for 150 students — her "right" pedagogy doesn't scale.
  • Worries & aspirations: burning out or being proven naive; becoming the school's (and eventually the region's) AI lead.
👂 Hear
  • Influencers: online TESOL and AI-in-education communities, edu-Twitter/TikTok — her real staffroom.
  • Boss: Fernando showcases her at open house but gives her no hours, tools, or policy cover.
  • Friends/colleagues: colleagues call her naive; Lourdes-types treat her as part of the problem.
👀 See
  • Environment: students using AI everywhere regardless of rules; a school brochure promising what classrooms don't deliver.
  • Peers: teachers next door banning what she permits — students exploiting the inconsistency.
  • Market offers: detectors she considers snake oil; nobody selling process visibility or scalable oral assessment.
🗣️ Say & do

  • In public: the school's slightly contrarian "AI teacher"; evangelizes, pilots everything, presents at staff meetings.
  • Appearance: young, digital-native, Notion-organized; grades voice notes on her commute.
🛑 Pain
  • Fears: her method collapsing under its own weight; being stuck between parents who call AI "the cheating machine" and a school that won't back her.
  • Frustrations: double prep and grading time; version-history spot-checks that are tedious and gameable; her class rules are unenforceable.
  • Obstacles: no budget authority, no institutional policy, no tooling for process-based assessment at scale.
🏆 Gain
  • Wants/needs: process-based, AI-resistant assessment that is sustainable; evidence her approach produces real gains.
  • Measures of success: hours back in her week; measurable language outcomes she can show dirección; recognition as the school's AI lead.
  • Obstacles to overcome: turning her one-classroom experiment into school-level practice — with data, not enthusiasm.

  • Get into a top university; nothing before that terminally matters.

Frustrations

Context

Santiago runs school like a portfolio. Every assignment gets a cost-benefit call: will effort here move my grade or my application? Essays, reports, reading reflections — AI, always, refined so it "sounds like him" (he keeps a prompt describing his own writing style). He does study for exams, because exams can't be outsourced — which to him proves the system: what can be automated deserves to be.

Current behavior & workarounds

  • Prompt library per subject and teacher ("Lourdes flags fancy words — write at B2 level with two small grammar errors").
  • Sells "homework help" (his prompts) to classmates informally.
  • Studies genuinely only for exam-weighted material — a rational reading of Andrés-style grading policies.

Tech stack & influence

  • Uses: ChatGPT Plus, WhatsApp group chats (prompt-sharing economy), Google Docs, Photomath.
  • Adoption role: Adversary. He will actively probe and route around any integrity tool; if he beats it, the whole class knows by Friday. A product that survives Santiago is credible.

Assumptions to validate

  1. Heavy bypassers are a minority, but their prompt-sharing sets the norms for everyone else (network effect of cheating).
  2. Even hardened bypassers engage honestly when grading shifts to synchronous/oral formats — resentfully but compliantly.
  3. The suppressed "my GPA is a bubble" anxiety is real and reachable (a wedge for products that make learning visible rather than punish).

👂 Hear

  • Boss/authority: teachers' vague warnings about AI — he discounts them because they can never prove anything.
  • Friends: prompt-sharing group chats where he is the acknowledged expert; classmates who pay for his "homework help."
👀 See
  • Environment: a school that grades documents, not learning; assessment systems full of exploitable gaps.
  • Peers: everyone using AI at different skill levels; the skilled ones thriving.
  • Market offers: ever-better AI models and tools; detectors he has personally beaten.
🗣️ Say & do
  • Toward others: charming and helpful — sells prompts, performs diligence to teachers, wins confrontations politely.
  • In public: the model student — extracurriculars that "look good," offended (performatively) by unproven accusations.
  • Appearance: polished, confident, curated — like his essays, tuned to "sound like him."
🛑 Pain
  • Fears: getting caught with proof before applications close; synchronous formats (oral defenses, in-class writing) exposing the gap between his GPA and his knowledge.

  • Measures of success: top GPA, the admission letter, peer status as the guy who beat the system.

  1. Selective users are the largest student segment, and their triage line is perceived assignment value, not difficulty or subject.
  2. Assignment redesign (make it feel worth doing) moves Valeria more than enforcement does.

How the nine personas interlock

## How the nine personas interlock

The classroom triangle (users × actors):

  • Lourdes (resister) is in an arms race with Santiago (bypasser) — and her collateral damage is Rodrigo (falsely suspected). Her retreat-to-paper punishes Valeria's legitimate time constraints too.
  • Andrés (pragmatist) has already conceded homework to Santiago and Valeria's triage; his pain is not knowing who's practicing until exams expose them.
  • Renata (embracer) has the pedagogy that would convert Valeria, protect Rodrigo, and constrain Santiago — but it doesn't scale without tooling.

The buying triangle (buyers × everyone):

  • Beatriz (prohibitionist) writes the policy Lourdes wants and can't enforce it; Santiago is her scandal risk, Rodrigo's parents her legal risk.
  • Fernando (innovator) showcases Renata but hasn't equipped her; his brochure-reality gap is exposed the day a parent asks Valeria how school "teaches AI responsibly."
  • Patricia (skeptic) will only buy what Renata demands and Lourdes tolerates — she is the adoption test the whole market imposes.

The strategic hypothesis (carried over, now sharpened): detection is a dead end — unreliable, adversarial versus Santiago, legally radioactive versus Rodrigo, already distrusted by Lourdes, Beatriz, and Patricia alike. The aligned frame is making learning visible: process evidence and assessment formats where honest effort is provable (Rodrigo), worthwhile (Valeria), scalable to grade (Renata, Andrés), enforceable (Lourdes, Beatriz), marketable (Fernando), and demonstrably adopted (Patricia). Every persona's incentives can align on that frame; none align on better catching.

Sharpest cross-persona tension to resolve in discovery: Beatriz will not buy anything that "embraces AI"; Fernando will not buy anything that smells like prohibition. Can one product serve both with different framing, or is this two segments and we must choose our beachhead?
